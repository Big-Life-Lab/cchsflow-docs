# Technical Report: Harmonization of Canadian Community Health Survey (CCHS) Data through the cchsflow Architecture and 613apps Ecosystem

## Executive Summary

The Canadian Community Health Survey (CCHS) stands as a critical instrument in the national surveillance of health determinants, health status, and healthcare utilization. Since its inception in 2000, the CCHS has provided the granular data necessary for health region-level analysis, supporting thousands of peer-reviewed studies and policy decisions. However, the longitudinal utility of this data is significantly constrained by methodological heterogeneity across survey cycles—a phenomenon known as "concept drift." Changes in variable nomenclature, question phrasing, response categorization, and skip-pattern logic create substantial barriers to multi-cycle analysis, often necessitating labor-intensive and error-prone manual harmonization.

To address these systemic challenges, the Big Life Lab at the Ottawa Hospital Research Institute has developed a comprehensive open-science infrastructure comprising the `cchsflow` R package and the `613apps.ca` web ecosystem. This report provides an exhaustive technical analysis of this infrastructure, with a specific focus on the "PLAN_database_rebuild" architectural model. This model represents a paradigm shift from ad-hoc data cleaning to a metadata-driven, reproducible workflow. By decoupling variable definition from transformation logic using a dual-table CSV schema (`variables.csv` and `variable_details.csv`), the architecture enables the standardized processing of over 330 variables across 18 years of survey data.

The report further examines the role of the `rec_with_table()` function as the central processing engine, the integration of `tagged_na` for nuanced missing data handling, and the complementary role of `613apps.ca` as a visualization and metadata exploration layer. Through detailed case studies of complex variables such as Body Mass Index (BMI) and physical activity, and a comparative analysis with international standards like the Data Documentation Initiative (DDI) and Maelstrom Research guidelines, this document establishes the `cchsflow` ecosystem as a robust, scalable solution for population health data harmonization.

## 1. Introduction: The Imperative of Data Harmonization

### 1.1 The Landscape of Population Health Surveillance

Population health surveillance relies on the continuous, systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of health-related data. In Canada, the CCHS is the flagship survey for this purpose, administered by Statistics Canada. It collects cross-sectional information from a large sample of respondents—ranging from 65,000 to 130,000 per cycle—to provide reliable estimates at sub-provincial levels.^^ The survey covers a broad spectrum of topics, including chronic conditions, mental health, lifestyle behaviors (smoking, alcohol use, physical activity), and sociodemographic characteristics.

Since the first cycle in 2000/2001, the CCHS has undergone significant evolution to remain relevant to emerging public health issues. The survey frequency shifted from a biennial collection (2001, 2003, 2005) to an annual collection in 2007, and recently returned to a two-year release cycle.^^ While these changes enhance the survey's responsiveness—allowing for the rapid inclusion of questions on vaping or pandemic-related behaviors—they introduce substantial complexity for longitudinal analysis.

### 1.2 The Challenge of Concept Drift

For researchers attempting to analyze trends over time, the CCHS presents a fragmented landscape. The core challenge is "concept drift," where the operational definition or measurement of a variable changes between cycles. This discordance manifests in three primary dimensions:

1. **Nomenclature Instability:** Variable names are frequently altered by Statistics Canada to reflect minor changes in question wording or module placement. For instance, the variable for "Sex" appeared as `DHHA_SEX` in 2001 but as `DHH_SEX` in 2007.^^ While semantically identical, these discrepancies break automated analysis scripts, forcing researchers to manually map names across files.
2. **Categorization Shifts:** The granularity of response categories often fluctuates. A variable measuring the frequency of alcohol consumption might use a 5-point scale in one cycle and a 7-point scale in another. Harmonizing these requires mapping both scales to a common denominator, a process that demands deep knowledge of the survey metadata to avoid misclassification bias.^^
3. **Measurement Protocol Changes:** Anthropometric data provides a stark example of protocol shifts. In early cycles (2001, 2003), height and weight were often collected in imperial units (inches/pounds) and subject to specific rounding or capping rules (e.g., capping height at 76 inches). In later cycles, metric units became the standard, and capping rules changed.^^ Naive combination of these datasets without rigorous transformation logic leads to systematic measurement error.

Prior to the development of `cchsflow`, the standard approach to these problems was the creation of project-specific "cleaning scripts," typically written in SAS or SPSS. These scripts were rarely shared, leading to a duplication of effort and a lack of standardization. A researcher in British Columbia might harmonize "Physical Activity" differently than a researcher in Nova Scotia, rendering their findings incomparable.

### 1.3 The Harmonization Solution

The `cchsflow` package and the `613apps.ca` platform emerged as a response to this fragmentation. By centralizing the harmonization logic in an open-source repository and adopting a metadata-driven architecture, the project aims to create a "single source of truth" for CCHS data transformation. This approach aligns with broader movements in epidemiology toward reproducibility and open science, ensuring that data cleaning—often the most time-consuming and opaque part of the research pipeline—becomes transparent and verifiable.

## 2. Theoretical Framework: Approaches to Retrospective Harmonization

To understand the architectural decisions behind `cchsflow`, it is necessary to situate it within the broader theoretical framework of data harmonization. Harmonization can be prospective (standardizing collection methods before data is gathered) or retrospective (standardizing data after collection). The CCHS context is strictly retrospective.

### 2.1 The Maelstrom Research Guidelines

Maelstrom Research has established widely accepted guidelines for retrospective data harmonization, which emphasize a rigorous, iterative process to ensure quality and reproducibility.^^ These guidelines propose a workflow involving:

1. **Define Research Question:** Clearly articulate the objectives.
2. **Assemble Information:** Gather documentation (codebooks, questionnaires) for all study waves.
3. **Define Variables (DataSchema):** Create a core set of target variables to be harmonized.
4. **Evaluate Potential:** Assess the feasibility of mapping source variables to target variables.
5. **Process Data:** Develop and execute transformation algorithms.
6. **Validate:** Compare harmonized outputs against source data distributions.

The `cchsflow` architecture, specifically the "PLAN_database_rebuild," essentially automates steps 3 through 5 of this framework. The `variables.csv` file serves as the  **DataSchema** , defining the core variables. The `variable_details.csv` file encapsulates the **Evaluation of Potential** and  **Processing logic** , codifying the specific rules for mapping source to target.

### 2.2 The Data Documentation Initiative (DDI)

The DDI is an international standard for describing data from the social, behavioral, and economic sciences.^^ It provides a structured XML schema for documenting variable-level metadata, including question text, response categories, and skip patterns. While DDI is excellent for archival purposes, its complexity and XML verbosity make it difficult for average researchers to use directly in analysis workflows.

`cchsflow` adopts a pragmatic alternative to DDI. Instead of heavy XML, it uses tidy, flat CSV files that integrate natively with the R ecosystem (specifically the `tidyverse`). This "DDI-lite" approach lowers the barrier to entry, allowing researchers to interact with metadata using standard dataframe operations while still maintaining the structured rigor required for harmonization.^^

## 3. The PLAN Architecture: A Metadata-Driven Database Rebuild

The core of the `cchsflow` system is the "PLAN_database_rebuild" architecture. This design philosophy prioritizes the separation of *logic* from  *code* . In traditional scripts, transformation rules (e.g., `if year == 2001 then height = height * 0.0254`) are hard-coded into the execution flow. In the PLAN architecture, these rules are abstracted into external metadata files, making the system modular, extensible, and easier to audit.

### 3.1 The Dual-Table Schema

The architecture relies on two primary CSV specification files that function as a relational database for harmonization logic.

#### 3.1.1 The Variable Master List (`variables.csv`)

This file acts as the registry for the ecosystem, defining the "target" state of the harmonized data. It contains metadata for over 330 variables across domains such as demography, health behaviors, and chronic conditions.^^

**Table 1: Schema of `variables.csv`**

| **Column Name** | **Description**                                          | **Functional Role**                                              |
| --------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `variable`          | The standardized variable name (e.g.,`DHH_SEX`,`HWTGBMI`). | Primary Key. Acts as the unique identifier for the harmonized concept. |
| `label`             | Short label.                                                   | Used for quick reference in R outputs.                                 |
| `labelLong`         | Detailed descriptive label.                                    | Used for publication-quality tables and graphs.                        |
| `section`           | Domain category (e.g., "Demography").                          | Facilitates filtering and navigation in `613apps.ca`.                |
| `subject`           | Specific topic (e.g., "Sex", "BMI").                           | Grouping variable for related measures.                                |
| `variableType`      | Data type (`cat`for categorical,`cont`for continuous).     | Determines valid statistical operations and QC checks.                 |
| `units`             | Unit of measurement (e.g., "kg", "meters").                    | Critical for physical measures to prevent unit errors.                 |
| `databaseStart`     | List of supported cycles (e.g.,`cchs2001_p, cchs2003_p`).    | inventory control; tells the user which cycles contain the variable.   |
| `variableStart`     | List of original variable names.                               | Traceability; maps the target back to its source lineage.              |

This file serves a strategic function: it allows for rapid "gap analysis." A researcher can query this table to immediately see which variables are available for a specific time range (e.g., "Is 'Vaping' available in the 2010 cycle?"), without needing to load the massive microdata files.

#### 3.1.2 The Transformation Logic (`variable_details.csv`)

While `variables.csv` defines *what* the variables are, `variable_details.csv` defines *how* to generate them. This file contains the granular transformation logic, with over 3,200 rows of specific mapping rules.^^ It uses a long-format structure where each row represents a single category transformation for a specific variable in a specific survey cycle.

**Table 2: Schema of `variable_details.csv`**

| **Column**  | **Description**                                             | **Example**                     |
| ----------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| `variable`      | Foreign key to `variables.csv`.                                 | `DHH_SEX`                           |
| `databaseStart` | The specific survey cycle being processed.                        | `cchs2001_p`                        |
| `variableStart` | The name of the variable in the source data.                      | `DHHA_SEX`                          |
| `recStart`      | The value or range in the source data to be mapped.               | `1`(for Male), `` (for range)       |
| `recEnd`        | The target value in the harmonized data.                          | `1`                                 |
| `typeStart`     | The data type of the source variable.                             | `cat`                               |
| `typeEnd`       | The data type of the target variable.                             | `cat`                               |
| `func`          | Name of a custom R function to execute (for complex derivations). | `bmi_fun`                           |
| `notes`         | Documentation of specific issues or decisions.                    | "Variable name changed from DHHA_SEX" |

**Key Architectural Features:**

* **Interval Notation:** The `recStart` column uses mathematical interval notation (e.g., `[0, 10)`, ``) to define input ranges. This allows for the robust handling of both continuous variables (e.g., binning age into groups) and discrete categories. A range of `[1, 2.5]` captures all values between 1 and 2.5 inclusive, providing precision that simple equality operators lack.^^
* **Else Logic:** Every variable definition implies an `else` condition. Values not explicitly matched by a `recStart` rule are typically mapped to `NA` or a specific "Else" category. This "defensive programming" ensures that unexpected data points (e.g., new outlier codes) do not silently pass through as valid data.
* **Function Hooks:** The `func` column allows the static CSV to trigger dynamic R code. If a transformation is too complex to be expressed as a simple map (e.g., it requires arithmetic operations on multiple variables), the CSV points to a named function (e.g., `bmi_fun`) stored in the package. The `rec_with_table` engine then executes this function.^^

### 3.2 Advantages of the PLAN Architecture

The transition to this file-based architecture offers several distinct advantages over traditional database systems or hard-coded scripts:

1. **Version Control:** Because the logic is stored in plain text CSV files, it can be tracked using Git. Every change to a variable definition—no matter how small—is recorded in the commit history, providing a complete audit trail of the harmonization logic.
2. **Community Contribution:** The CSV format lowers the barrier to entry for contributors. An epidemiologist without advanced R programming skills can still contribute to the project by adding a new variable to the `variables.csv` file or correcting a mapping in `variable_details.csv` via a simple Pull Request.^^
3. **Language Agnosticism:** While `cchsflow` is an R package, the metadata is language-neutral. The logic encoded in `variable_details.csv` (source variable X maps to target Y using rule Z) could theoretically be read by a Python script or a SAS macro, enabling cross-platform harmonization.^^

## 4. The Transformation Engine: `rec_with_table()`

The operational heart of the `cchsflow` package is the `rec_with_table()` function. This function acts as the interpreter for the "PLAN" architecture, reading the specifications and executing the transformations on the raw data.

### 4.1 Algorithmic Workflow

The function follows a strict sequence of operations to ensure data integrity and transparency ^^:

1. **Initialization:** The user provides the raw dataset (e.g., `cchs2001_p`) and a list of desired variables (or defaults to all).
2. **Schema Loading:** The function loads the `variable_details.csv` and `variables.csv` files, either from the package installation or a local override.
3. **Filter and Validation:** It filters the schema to include only the rows relevant to the input `databaseStart` (the cycle) and the requested variables. It validates that the source variables specified in `variableStart` actually exist in the input dataframe.
4. **Transformation Loop:**
   * For **Simple Recodes:** It iterates through the rows, identifying input values matching `recStart` and replacing them with `recEnd`.
   * For **Complex Derivations:** If the `func` column is populated, it retrieves the specified function (e.g., `bmi_fun`) and the necessary source variables (e.g., height and weight). It then passes the data subset to that function for processing.
5. **Data Type Enforcement:** It ensures the output variable matches the `typeEnd` specification (e.g., coercing to factor or numeric).
6. **Labeling:** It applies the `label` and `labelLong` metadata to the output dataframe, ensuring the data is self-documenting.
7. **Logging:** Throughout the process, it generates a verbose log (unless suppressed). This log details every transformation occurred, providing a critical audit trail for the researcher. For example:  *"The variable DHHA_SEX was recoded into DHH_SEX for database cchs2001_p... Value 1 mapped to 1, Value 2 mapped to 2"* .^^

### 4.2 Handling Missing Data: The `tagged_na` Innovation

One of the most significant technical achievements of `cchsflow` is its nuanced handling of missing data. In standard statistical software, "missing" is often a binary state: data is either present or it is `NA`. However, in survey methodology, the concept of "missing" is heterogeneous.

The CCHS uses specific codes to denote different types of non-response:

* **Code 6:** Valid Skip / Not Applicable (e.g., a question about pregnancy asking a male respondent).
* **Code 7:** Don't Know.
* **Code 8:** Refusal.
* **Code 9:** Not Stated.

Collapsing these distinct states into a single `NA` value results in information loss and potential statistical bias. For instance, treating "Valid Skip" (Code 6) as "Missing" (Code 7/8/9) would incorrectly inflate the missing data rate and could distort denominator calculations for prevalence estimates.

`cchsflow` leverages the `tagged_na` functionality from the `haven` package (part of the tidyverse) to preserve this distinction.^^

* **`NA(a)`:** Assigned to "Not Applicable" (Code 6).
* **`NA(b)`:** Assigned to "Missing" (Codes 7, 8, 9).

This allows researchers to write precise inclusion/exclusion logic. When calculating smoking rates, a researcher can easily filter out `NA(a)` respondents (children) from the denominator, while deciding how to handle `NA(b)` respondents (adults who refused to answer) based on their specific sensitivity analysis plan.

## 5. The 613apps Ecosystem and Visualization Layer

While `cchsflow` provides the computational backend, the `613apps.ca` website serves as the accessibility layer. Developed and maintained by the Big Life Lab (including key contributor Joel Barnes), this ecosystem bridges the gap between the technical metadata and the end-user.^^

### 5.1 The Data Dictionary Builder

The flagship tool on `613apps.ca` is the  **Data Dictionary Builder** . This R Shiny web application acts as a graphical user interface (GUI) for the `variables.csv` and `variable_details.csv` files.

**Functionality:**

* **Metadata Exploration:** Users can search for variables by keyword (e.g., "depression", "fruit intake") across all 18 years of CCHS data simultaneously. This replaces the tedious process of manually searching through dozens of PDF codebooks from Statistics Canada.
* **Visualizing Logic:** The tool visualizes the harmonization lineage. A user can select a variable like "Smoking Status" and see exactly how it is constructed in each cycle—which source variables are used, and how categories are mapped.
* **Cohort Planning:** Researchers planning a study can use the tool to identify which cycles support their variables of interest, facilitating feasibility assessments before purchasing or accessing the data.

### 5.2 Integration with Other Health Data Tools

The `613apps` ecosystem extends beyond the CCHS. It also hosts tools related to the  **Canadian Health Measures Survey (CHMS)** , supported by the `chms` R package.^^ This suggests a broader strategic vision at the Big Life Lab: creating a suite of interoperable, open-source tools for Canadian health data. By sharing a common architectural DNA (R packages + Shiny frontends + Metadata CSVs), these tools reduce the cognitive load for researchers working across different Statistics Canada surveys.

The platform also supports  **Population Health Modeling (POHEM)** , referencing tools that forecast health risks like obesity and diabetes. The harmonization provided by `cchsflow` is a prerequisite for such modeling, as these models require consistent historical data to train predictive algorithms.^^

## 6. Case Studies in Harmonization

To fully appreciate the robustness of the `cchsflow` architecture, it is instructive to examine specific examples of variable harmonization.

### 6.1 Case Study 1: Body Mass Index (BMI)

BMI is a quintessential example of a variable prone to concept drift. It is a derived variable calculated from height and weight.

* **The Discordance:**
  * **2001 (Cycle 1.1):** Height was recorded in the variable `HWTAGHT` using inches. Critically, values were capped: any height over 74 inches was recorded as a single category, necessitating a conversion logic (e.g., setting 74+ to 76 inches) to avoid ceiling effects. Weight (`HWTAGWTK`) was in kilograms.
  * **2014 (Annual):** Height was recorded in meters, with different variable names and no such imperial capping.
* **The Harmonization Logic:**
  * The `variables.csv` defines the target variable `HWTGBMI`.
  * The `variable_details.csv` for 2001 points to the `bmi_fun` function.
  * The `rec_with_table` engine executes `bmi_fun`. This function contains the specific logic to:
    1. Convert 2001 height from inches to meters.
    2. Apply the specific adjustment for the "74+ inches" cap (mapping it to 76 inches/1.93 meters).
    3. Calculate **$BMI = Weight / Height^2$**.
    4. Round the result to the standard precision.
* **Outcome:** A harmonized BMI variable that is methodologically consistent across all cycles, despite the underlying differences in data collection.^^

### 6.2 Case Study 2: Physical Activity

Physical activity variables are notoriously difficult to harmonize due to changing international guidelines and measurement instruments.

* **The Discordance:** CCHS cycles have varied between inquiring about specific activities (frequency and duration of walking, swimming, etc.) versus global physical activity levels. Furthermore, the definition of "Active" vs. "Inactive" often depends on calculated Energy Expenditure (EE) values derived from these responses.
* **The Harmonization Logic:** `cchsflow` standardizes these into derived metrics like the Physical Activity Index (`PACDPAI`). The system uses `rec_with_table` to aggregate the various activity-specific inputs into a total daily energy expenditure (KKD) and then categorizes respondents into "Active", "Moderately Active", and "Inactive" based on thresholds stored in the CSV metadata.
* **Benefit:** By storing the thresholds in the CSV, the definition of "Active" is transparent. If health guidelines change, the definition can be updated in one place (the CSV) rather than in hundreds of dispersed scripts.

### 6.3 Case Study 3: Smoking

Smoking variables involve complex skip patterns that vary by cycle.

* **The Discordance:** Questions about "Type of Smoker" (Daily, Occasional, Non-smoker) are only asked of respondents who define themselves as current smokers. The path to this question varies: some cycles ask "Have you ever smoked?" first; others ask "Do you smoke now?"
* **The Harmonization Logic:** The `cchsflow` logic ensures that the "Universe" (the population to whom the question applies) is consistent. It maps the various "Valid Skip" pathways to the standardized `NA(a)` tag. This ensures that when an analyst calculates smoking prevalence, the denominator correctly represents the intended population (e.g., all adults), without inadvertently excluding valid non-smokers or including those who were not asked.^^

## 7. Operational Workflow and Best Practices

### 7.1 Installation and Setup

The `cchsflow` package is available via CRAN and GitHub, reflecting its status as a mature, production-ready tool. Users can install it using standard R commands:

**R**

```
install.packages("cchsflow")
# or for the latest development version
devtools::install_github("Big-Life-Lab/cchsflow")
```

This ease of access is a key component of the project's impact strategy, ensuring that the tools are available to any researcher with an internet connection.^^

### 7.2 The Recommended Harmonization Workflow

To maximize the benefits of the architecture, researchers are advised to follow a specific workflow:

1. **Consult the Dictionary:** Use `613apps.ca` to identify the variables relevant to the research question and verify their availability across the desired years.
2. **Clone the Variables Sheet:** Copy the `variables.csv` template from the package to a local project folder. Filter it to include only the variables needed for the study. This creates a "project-specific schema."
3. **Customize (Optional):** If a variable is missing or requires a custom definition, add it to the local `variables.csv` and `variable_details.csv`.
4. **Execute Transformation:** Use `rec_with_table()` to process each survey cycle (PUMF or Share file) independently.
5. **Concatenate:** Use `dplyr::bind_rows()` to combine the harmonized datasets into a single longitudinal file.
6. **Verify:** Check the logs generated by `rec_with_table()` to ensure no unexpected data loss occurred.

### 7.3 Community Contribution and Open Science

The "PLAN" architecture relies on community maintenance. Because the logic is open-source, the Big Life Lab encourages users to contribute back to the ecosystem. If a researcher harmonizes a new variable for their study, they can submit a Pull Request to the GitHub repository to add that variable to the master `variables.csv`.^^ This crowdsourcing model ensures that the library grows over time, distributing the burden of maintenance across the entire Canadian health research community.

## 8. Conclusion and Strategic Outlook

The `cchsflow` package and the `613apps.ca` ecosystem represent a significant advancement in the field of population health data science. By transitioning from a fragmented, script-based approach to a centralized, metadata-driven architecture (the "PLAN_database_rebuild"), the Big Life Lab has solved the persistent problem of concept drift in the Canadian Community Health Survey.

The technical innovations of this system—specifically the dual-table CSV schema, the `rec_with_table` engine, and the `tagged_na` missing data handling—provide a robust foundation for reproducible research. Furthermore, the integration with the `613apps` web platform democratizes access to this complex metadata, bridging the gap between technical data engineering and applied public health policy.

As the CCHS continues to evolve, the flexible nature of the `cchsflow` architecture ensures it can adapt to future changes. The decoupling of logic from code allows for rapid updates to harmonization rules without requiring core software changes. Ultimately, this infrastructure not only saves thousands of hours of analyst time but improves the quality and comparability of health evidence in Canada, ensuring that policy decisions are based on rigorous, standardized data.
